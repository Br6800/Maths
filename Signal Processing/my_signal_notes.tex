\section*{Chapter 3}
We assume a certain signal $x(t)$ has the form $$
x(t) = \sum_{\Z} a_k e^{kw_0tj},
$$
where $j \in \C$. The Fourier coefficients follow on to be $$
a_k = \frac{1}{T} \int_{\R} x(t)e^{-jw_0kt}dt.
$$
There's a nice way to manipulate the product of $x(t)y(t)$ to determine Fourier coefficients.
We have $$
x(t)y(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\sum_{\ell = -\infty}^{\infty} a_kb_{\ell} e^{jw_0(k+\ell)t}
$$
by a simple rearrangement. We can find the coefficients of the $m^{\text{\tiny th}}$ harmonic via restricting $k=m-\ell$ to get the Fourier coefficients of the product:
$$
h_m = \sum_{\ell = -\infty}^{\infty} a_{m-\ell}b_{\ell}.
$$
